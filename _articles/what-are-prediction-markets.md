# What Are Prediction Markets? Complete Guide

In 1906, at a county fair in Plymouth, England, scientist Francis Galton watched 787 people guess the weight of an ox. Farmers, butchers, and random townspeople all submitted their estimates. Galton expected the crowd to fail spectacularly. Instead, the median guess landed at 1,197 pounds. The ox weighed 1,198 pounds. Less than one percent deviation from 787 independent guesses.

This experiment demonstrated something counterintuitive: crowds can outperform experts when their judgments remain independent and diverse. That single insight has grown into a multi-billion dollar industry. In 2024 alone, Polymarket processed $9 billion in trading volume, with $3.7 billion wagered on the U.S. presidential election. Monthly prediction market volume grew 130-fold between early 2024 and 2025.

Prediction markets convert scattered private information into probability signals. They work because people with real knowledge have financial reasons to trade on what they know rather than what sounds good to say. The result: continuously-updated forecasts that documented research shows outperform expert opinions, polls, and internal corporate projections.

## What Is a Prediction Market?

A **prediction market** is a trading platform where contracts pay out based on whether specific events occur. Traders buy and sell shares tied to outcomes, and prices reflect the crowd's aggregated probability assessment for each scenario.

The core mechanic is straightforward. If you believe an event has a higher probability than the current market price suggests, you buy. If you think the probability is lower, you sell. When the event resolves, winning contracts pay $1 and losing contracts pay nothing.

Consider a contract priced at $0.65 for "Candidate X wins the election." That price means the market collectively estimates a 65% probability of Candidate X winning. If you hold strong information suggesting the true probability is 80%, buying at $0.65 gives you positive expected value. Your purchase pushes the price up slightly, incorporating your private information into the public signal.

| Method | How It Works | Information Source | Incentive |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Prediction Markets** | Trade contracts on outcomes | Dispersed private knowledge | Financial profit from accuracy |
| **Polls/Surveys** | Ask people their opinions | Stated preferences | None (or small compensation) |
| **Expert Forecasts** | Individual specialists make projections | Expert knowledge and models | Reputation (indirect) |
| **Betting/Gambling** | Wager against the house or other bettors | Varies | Entertainment plus profit |

The key distinction is the incentive structure. Prediction markets reward accuracy with money. People who know something trade on it. People who guess wrong lose capital and eventually stop trading. Over time, this selection pressure means prices reflect the best available information.

### How Prediction Markets Aggregate Information

The statistical foundation traces back to Galton's ox and was formalized by James Surowiecki in his 2004 book *The Wisdom of Crowds*. When individual errors are uncorrelated, they cancel out. If one person overestimates by 10% and another underestimates by 10%, the average lands closer to truth than either individual guess.

Three conditions enable crowd accuracy:

**Diversity of opinion**: Participants bring different backgrounds, information sources, and analytical frameworks. Homogeneous crowds make correlated errors.

**Independence**: Each person forms their judgment without being overly influenced by others. Herding behavior creates bubbles.

**Decentralization**: Knowledge is distributed, not centralized. No single authority dictates the "correct" view.

Financial stakes amplify this effect. In surveys, people can say whatever they want with no consequences. In prediction markets, money separates genuine belief from cheap talk. Research published in 2006 demonstrated that real-money prediction markets significantly outperform play-money markets for non-sports events.

### Binary Outcomes vs Continuous Markets

**Binary markets** resolve yes or no. "Will the incumbent win?" Either they win and YES contracts pay $1, or they lose and NO contracts pay $1.

**Continuous markets** (also called vote-share markets) pay based on percentage outcomes. "What will the incumbent's vote share be?" A contract might pay $0.01 for each percentage point of the final vote share.

The **Iowa Electronic Markets** has run both types since 1988. Their vote-share markets tend to show even higher accuracy than their winner-take-all markets because they aggregate more granular information about expected margins.

## Why Prediction Markets Work

The conventional approach to forecasting puts faith in finding the right expert or building the right model. This approach fails for predictable reasons.

Individual experts carry systematic biases. They anchor on their prior views, overweight recent information, and have incentives to make bold predictions that get attention rather than accurate ones. Quantitative models depend on historical patterns, which break during novel situations. Neither approach adapts well to new information arriving in real-time.

Prediction markets solve these problems through structure, not talent. The mechanism doesn't require any single participant to be brilliant. It requires diversity, independence, and stakes.

### The Wisdom of Crowds Effect

Galton's ox experiment demonstrated the core principle: aggregated independent judgments converge toward accuracy. If individual errors are randomly distributed (some too high, some too low), they wash out in aggregate.

This only works when the three conditions hold. Break any of them and crowds become mobs:

**Groupthink** destroys independence. The 2008 financial crisis showed what happens when an entire market believes the same narrative about housing prices.

**Lack of diversity** creates blind spots. Markets dominated by a single type of trader lose the error-cancellation benefit.

**Centralization** replaces crowd wisdom with individual judgment. If traders defer to a single authority or influencer, you're back to expert forecasting with extra steps.

The Best Buy case illustrates positive conditions. When the company used an internal prediction market with employees across different roles and departments, the crowd predicted sales with 99.5% accuracy compared to 5% error in official executive forecasts.

### Financial Incentives and Information Revelation

Money changes behavior. In a survey about election outcomes, a respondent might say their preferred candidate will win because it feels good. No consequences for being wrong.

In a prediction market, that same person faces a choice: bet with their feelings and potentially lose money, or bet with their honest probability assessment and potentially profit. Real stakes force honest evaluation.

Financial incentives surface information that wouldn't otherwise reach forecasts. A regional campaign staffer who notices enthusiasm flagging in key precincts has no way to communicate that to pollsters. But they can trade on it. Internal prediction markets at companies like Eli Lilly allowed pharmaceutical researchers to signal concerns about drug trial outcomes before official results.

The information revelation function explains why prediction markets beat polls even when sampling the same population. Polls measure what people say. Markets measure what people are willing to stake money on.

### Price Discovery and Probability Signals

Traditional forecasts are point-in-time snapshots. A poll taken today tells you what people thought when the poll was conducted. By the time results are published, reality may have shifted.

Prediction market prices update continuously. New information arrives, traders adjust positions, prices move. During the 2024 election cycle, Polymarket prices responded within minutes to debate performances and campaign developments. Polls, by contrast, took days or weeks to reflect changing conditions.

## The History of Prediction Markets

### From County Fairs to Electronic Trading

Galton published his findings in 1907, treating the experiment as a curiosity about crowd psychology. The theoretical foundation solidified through work on market efficiency. If prices reflect all available information, then market prices themselves become valuable signals. Researchers began asking whether this could apply beyond financial assets to discrete events with verifiable outcomes.

### Iowa Electronic Markets (1988-Present)

The **Iowa Electronic Markets** (IEM), launched in 1988 at the University of Iowa, became the first modern electronic prediction market. Operating under a regulatory no-action letter from the CFTC, IEM allowed real-money trading on political outcomes for research purposes.

The results were striking. Across presidential elections from 1988 to 2020, IEM's average error was just 1.34 percentage points. Research by Berg, Nelson, and Rietz published in 2008 found that IEM beat polls 74% of the time, with the advantage growing at longer time horizons.

### The Modern Era: Polymarket, Kalshi, and PredictIt

The 2020s brought prediction markets from academic curiosity to mainstream financial infrastructure.

**PredictIt**, launched in 2014 under a CFTC no-action letter, allowed U.S. traders to participate in political markets with position limits. During the 2024 election, PredictIt markets correctly predicted 93% of outcomes according to analysis by Clinton and Huang at Vanderbilt.

**Kalshi**, founded in 2018 and fully CFTC-regulated in 2020, expanded into economic and event-based contracts. By 2025, the platform achieved an $11 billion valuation.

**Polymarket**, operating on cryptocurrency rails, processed $9 billion in volume during 2024. The platform handled $3.7 billion on the U.S. presidential election alone. In October 2025, Polymarket reached $4.1 billion in monthly volume. In 2025, Polymarket received CFTC approval and returned to the U.S. market, with Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) investing $2 billion at a $9 billion valuation.

Monthly prediction market volume grew 130x between early 2024 and late 2025. What was once a niche academic tool has become a significant financial market.

## Prediction Market Accuracy: What the Research Shows

Claims about prediction market superiority need evidence. Decades of academic research provide that evidence across political, corporate, and entertainment domains.

**Brier scores** offer a standardized accuracy metric for probability forecasts. The scale runs from 0 (perfect) to 2 (worst possible), with lower numbers indicating better accuracy. A Brier score below 0.125 indicates good forecasting; below 0.1 indicates strong performance.

Polymarket's research on their own platform found a Brier score of 0.0581 for predictions made 12 hours before resolution. High-liquidity markets scored even better at 0.0256. These numbers put prediction markets among the most accurate forecasting methods available.

### Markets vs Expert Forecasts

A study by Cowgill, Wolfers, and Zitzewitz examining markets at Google, Ford, and an anonymous firm (Firm X) found markets reduced mean-squared error by up to 25% compared to official internal forecasts.

At Google, internal prediction markets made over 175,000 predictions with participation from more than 10,000 employees. The markets accurately forecast COVID impacts, engineering milestone completions, and technology trend adoption.

HP ran internal prediction market experiments from 1996 to 1999. In 6 of 8 cases, the market predictions proved more accurate than existing internal forecasts.

The Best Buy case remains striking: employee crowd prediction achieved 99.5% accuracy versus 5% error in executive forecasts.

### Markets vs Polls and Surveys

Berg, Nelson, and Rietz found markets beat polls 74% of the time across their sample. The advantage was largest when comparing forecasts made well before election day; markets incorporate new information faster.

A study of six U.S. elections from 1992 to 2012 found that combining prediction market signals with polling data reduced forecasting error by 16-59%. The approaches capture different information types: polls measure stated preferences, markets measure informed probability assessments. Together they outperform either alone.

Sports prediction markets show similar patterns. Research found market accuracy of 54.28% compared to 42.63% for expert tipsters.

### Corporate Internal Market Results

Google's internal markets identified systematic optimism bias in company forecasts. The prediction market, aggregating more skeptical private views, produced more realistic expectations.

Ford used markets to forecast car sales, finding that the distributed knowledge of sales teams, dealers, and analysts aggregated into more accurate projections than centralized forecasting models.

The Hollywood Stock Exchange, tracking movie and awards outcomes, has predicted Oscar winners with 90% accuracy over its operational history.

## How Prediction Markets Are Used Today

### Political and Event Forecasting

Political markets remain the highest-profile application. The $3.7 billion wagered on the 2024 U.S. presidential election via Polymarket represents unprecedented public engagement with probability-based political analysis.

Beyond elections, markets now cover geopolitical events, policy decisions, Supreme Court rulings, and international developments. The Hollywood Stock Exchange's 90% Oscar accuracy demonstrates that market mechanics work wherever participants have dispersed relevant knowledge.

### Corporate Decision Making

Internal prediction markets now operate at Google, Ford, GE, Chevron, and within the UK and Czech governments. Applications include:

- **Product launch timing**: Employees aggregate views on development status and market conditions
- **Sales forecasting**: Sales teams and analysts trade on ground-level knowledge
- **Project deadlines**: Engineers reveal honest assessments through trading rather than politically-motivated status reports
- **Strategic decisions**: Employees trade on private views about risks and opportunities

The common thread: prediction markets surface information that organizational hierarchies suppress. People tell their boss what the boss wants to hear. They trade on what they actually believe.

### Financial and Business Intelligence

M&A due diligence teams monitor markets for signals about deal completion probabilities. Investors use market prices as inputs to valuation models. Competitive intelligence analysts track markets for signals about competitor announcements and strategic moves.

## Prediction Market Limitations and Criticisms

### Liquidity and Participation Requirements

Markets need traders to function. Low-liquidity markets may not attract enough participants with relevant information to generate reliable signals.

Polymarket research found that even low-liquidity markets maintained Brier scores below 0.1, but accuracy improved significantly with volume. High-liquidity markets achieved 0.0256 compared to 0.0581 for the broader sample.

### Manipulation Concerns

The 2024 election cycle brought accusations of wash trading and manipulation on Polymarket. Large position holders can move prices, at least temporarily, creating false signals.

Academic research suggests manipulation effects are usually temporary. Informed traders recognize mispriced contracts as profit opportunities and trade against manipulators. But in low-liquidity markets, manipulation can persist longer.

Market architecture matters. Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule (LMSR) mechanisms outperform continuous double auction (CDA) structures in low-participation scenarios because they provide guaranteed liquidity and make manipulation more expensive.

### When Crowds Fail

Surowiecki's original work emphasized that wise crowds aren't the same as mobs or bubbles. When the conditions for accuracy break down, prediction markets can fail badly.

**Correlated information** creates problems. If all traders read the same news sources and follow the same analysts, their errors align rather than cancel.

**Herding behavior** destroys independence. If traders defer to prominent market participants or chase momentum, prices reflect consensus rather than aggregated private information.

The 2016 election prediction failures (across markets and polls alike) illustrated these dynamics. Most participants drew from similar information sources, creating correlated errors that didn't wash out.

## Prediction Markets for Web Traffic and SEO

The mechanics that predict elections can forecast web traffic. This isn't theoretical speculation; it's applied information aggregation.

### Applying Market Mechanics to Traffic Forecasting

Traditional web traffic forecasting relies on historical data (which breaks when algorithms update), competitor snapshots (point-in-time analysis that's outdated by the time it's actionable), or expert opinion (subject to individual biases).

Prediction market mechanics offer something different:

- **Real-time collective intelligence**: Prices update continuously as participants incorporate new information
- **Skin-in-the-game accuracy incentives**: Traders who make better forecasts profit; those who guess wrong lose capital
- **Distributed private information**: SEO professionals, industry insiders, and informed observers each hold pieces of knowledge that no single analyst possesses

An SEO professional who notices a competitor site preparing a major content push, or who spots early signals of an algorithm update, has information that matters for traffic forecasting. Prediction market mechanics give them a way to monetize that edge while simultaneously improving the public signal.

### Why Traditional SEO Forecasting Falls Short

**Algorithm updates** break historical patterns. Google's core updates regularly reshuffle rankings in ways that historical regression models can't predict.

**Competitive dynamics** are hidden. Your competitor's content roadmap and strategic pivots aren't visible until they execute. By then, the impact on your traffic is already happening.

**Industry shifts** change baseline assumptions. Seasonal patterns and user behavior evolve. Last year's December traffic pattern may not predict this year's.

Prediction markets aggregate dispersed private information that no single analyst possesses. The hedge fund analyst who tracks e-commerce trends, the technical SEO who spotted a crawl budget issue, the content strategist who knows about upcoming Google product changes, and the agency insider who heard about a major brand's new campaign all hold relevant information. Markets give them a mechanism to share it profitably.
